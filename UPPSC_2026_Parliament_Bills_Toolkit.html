Types of Bills — UPPSC Master Chart

① Ordinary Bill (साधारण विधेयक) — Article 107–108

DEFINITION

Any bill that is NOT a Money Bill or Constitutional Amendment Bill. Deals with ordinary legislation — law, order, administration.

Can introduce in either House No President recommendation needed Joint Sitting possible on deadlock
KEY PROCEDURE
  • Can be introduced in Lok Sabha OR Rajya Sabha
  • Can be introduced by Minister OR Private Member
  • If RS rejects → Deadlock → Joint Sitting (Art. 108)
  • President can return for reconsideration once
  • If Parliament passes again → President must give assent

② Money Bill (धन विधेयक) — Article 110

DEFINITION — Art. 110

A bill is a Money Bill if it deals solely with:

  • Tax — imposition, abolition, alteration, regulation
  • Borrowing by Government of India
  • Consolidated Fund of India — custody, payment
  • Contingency Fund of India
  • Appropriation of money from Consolidated Fund
  • Audit of accounts of Government
CRITICAL FACTS
  • Introduced ONLY in Lok Sabha
  • Requires prior recommendation of President
  • Rajya Sabha can only suggest amendments (within 14 days)
  • Lok Sabha MAY or MAY NOT accept RS suggestions
  • NO Joint Sitting — Lok Sabha is supreme
  • Speaker certifies a bill as Money Bill — final & binding
  • President cannot withhold assent (no return for reconsideration)
⚠️ Speaker's Certification — UPPSC Favourite

If any question arises whether a bill is a Money Bill or not, the decision of the Speaker of Lok Sabha is final. This decision cannot be challenged in any court. (Art. 110(3))

③ Financial Bill (वित्त विधेयक) — Two Types

TYPE A — Financial Bill (Category I) — Art. 117(1)
  • Contains Money Bill provisions + other matters
  • Introduced ONLY in Lok Sabha
  • Requires President's prior recommendation
  • But NOT certified as Money Bill by Speaker
  • Joint Sitting IS possible on deadlock
TYPE B — Financial Bill (Category II) — Art. 117(3)
  • Involves expenditure from Consolidated Fund (not taxes)
  • Can be introduced in either House
  • Requires President's recommendation only when taken up for consideration (not at introduction)
  • Otherwise treated like an Ordinary Bill
  • Joint Sitting possible

④ Constitutional Amendment Bill — Article 368

THREE TYPES OF AMENDMENT
  • Simple Majority — Art. 368 NOT required (e.g., admission of new states, creation of LCs)
  • Special Majority — 2/3rd of members present & voting + majority of total membership of each House
  • Special Majority + State Ratification — By at least 50% of state legislatures (for federal provisions)
SPECIAL FEATURES
  • Can be introduced in either House
  • NO Joint Sitting — both houses must separately pass
  • NO prior recommendation of President needed
  • President must give assent — cannot withhold (24th Amendment)
  • Private member can also introduce

Passage of Ordinary Bill — Step by Step
📌 Key Concept

Every bill passes through 3 Readings in each House. Total journey = Originating House → Second House → President. Maximum time for complete passage = depends on type.

🏛️ Stage I — Originating House (Lok Sabha or Rajya Sabha)
First Reading — Introduction (पहला वाचन)
Minister moves motion. Bill's title & objectives read. No discussion. After intro, bill is published in Gazette. No voting on content
Second Reading — Scrutiny (दूसरा वाचन)
Most important stage — 3 options: (a) Take into immediate consideration (b) Refer to Select Committee (c) Refer to Joint Committee of both Houses. Clause-by-clause discussion & voting. Heart of legislative process
Committee Stage (if referred)
Select/Joint Committee examines bill clause by clause, may take expert evidence, submits report to House.
Third Reading — Final Vote (तीसरा वाचन)
No amendments permitted. Bill accepted or rejected as a whole. Simple majority needed. Bill passed by Originating House. Vote on entire bill
🏛️ Stage II — Second House (दूसरा सदन)
Same Three Readings repeat
Bill goes through First, Second and Third Reading in the Second House. Bill may be passed without amendments, passed with amendments, or rejected / kept pending for 6 months.
If Second House amends the bill
Bill returns to Originating House. If Originating House agrees to amendments → Bill passed. If disagrees → Deadlock → President may call Joint Sitting (Art. 108).
If Second House rejects or delays 6 months
Treated as deadlock → President may call Joint Sitting. In Joint Sitting, Lok Sabha usually prevails due to larger numbers.
👑 Stage III — President's Assent (राष्ट्रपति की स्वीकृति)
President's 4 Options (Ordinary Bill)
(1) Give Assent → Bill becomes Act ✅
(2) Withhold Assent → Pocket Veto (no time limit) — rare
(3) Return for Reconsideration → Suspensive Veto (Art. 111) — Parliament can override by re-passing
(4) Once returned and Parliament re-passes → Must give assent
Bill becomes Act
After assent, bill becomes an Act. Comes into force from date of assent or specified date.

Parliamentary Procedures — Question Hour, Zero Hour, Budget
⏰ Question Hour (प्रश्न काल)
  • First hour of every sitting of Parliament
  • Starred Questions — Oral answer, supplementaries allowed (⭐)
  • Unstarred Questions — Written answer only, no supplementaries
  • Short Notice Questions — Less than 10 days notice, urgent matters
  • Questions to Private Members — Addressed to Private Member in charge of a bill/resolution
  • 10 days advance notice needed for starred/unstarred
🕐 Zero Hour (शून्य काल)
  • Immediately after Question Hour (12:00 noon)
  • NOT mentioned in Constitution or Rules
  • Indian innovation — started in 1962
  • Members raise urgent public matters without advance notice
  • No voting — only raising issues
  • Half-an-hour Discussion (after Zero Hour) for matters arising from Q&A
  • Calling Attention Motion, Adjournment Motion — urgent issues
💰 Budget Session — Art. 112–116
  • Annual Financial Statement (Art. 112) = Budget = Statement of estimated receipts & expenditure
  • Two parts: Railway Budget (merged 2017) + General Budget
  • Vote on Account — takes general grant in advance (up to 2 months) before full budget passed
  • Appropriation Bill — authorises withdrawal from Consolidated Fund (cannot be amended)
  • Finance Bill — gives effect to taxation proposals
  • Cut Motions — oppose Demand for Grants
📋 Types of Motions
  • Censure Motion — against specific ministers (can be passed, PM need not resign)
  • No-Confidence Motion — against entire Council of Ministers; if passed, govt must resign
  • Confidence Motion — moved by government to prove majority
  • Adjournment Motion — urgent public importance, needs 50 members' support
  • Privilege Motion — against minister for breach of Parliamentary privilege
  • Calling Attention — call minister's attention to urgent matter

Joint Sitting of Parliament — Article 108
📌 Article 108 — Joint Sitting (संयुक्त बैठक)

When a deadlock arises between Lok Sabha and Rajya Sabha on a bill, the President may summon a Joint Sitting of both Houses. Presided over by the Speaker of Lok Sabha.

⚡ When Joint Sitting is Called
  • Condition 1 — Rajya Sabha rejects the bill
  • Condition 2 — Rajya Sabha passes the bill with amendments which Lok Sabha does not accept
  • Condition 3 — Rajya Sabha does not pass the bill within 6 months of receiving it
  • After any of these, President may notify intention to summon Joint Sitting
🚫 When Joint Sitting is NOT possible
  • Money Bill — Lok Sabha is supreme, RS has no veto
  • Constitutional Amendment Bill (Art. 368) — Both houses must separately pass with special majority
  • Financial Bill (Category I) — debatable, but generally treated like Ordinary Bill
📊 Joint Sitting — Key Facts
  • Presided by Speaker, Lok Sabha (in his absence, Deputy Speaker)
  • Decision by simple majority of total members present & voting
  • Lok Sabha (543+2) vastly outnumbers RS (245) → Lok Sabha's view prevails
  • Only amendments made by RS may be moved in Joint Sitting — not new ones
  • Once President notifies, bill cannot be amended beyond what RS had amended
  • 3 Joint Sittings have been held in India so far:
  • 1961 — Dowry Prohibition Bill
  • 1978 — Banking Service Commission (Repeal) Bill
  • 2002 — Prevention of Terrorism Act (POTA)
  • Speaker can adjourn Joint Sitting if quorum not present

Constitutional Amendment — Article 368 Deep Dive
📌 Art. 368 — Amendment Procedure
Introduction
Can be introduced in either House by a Minister or Private Member. No prior permission of President needed.
Passage with Special Majority
Must be passed by each House separately by: (a) Majority of total membership of that House AND (b) Majority of not less than 2/3rd of members present & voting
State Ratification (if required)
For federal provisions — at least 50% of State Legislatures must ratify by simple majority.
President's Assent — MANDATORY
President must give assent. Cannot return for reconsideration (24th Amendment 1971 made this mandatory).
🗂️ Three Categories of Amendment
① Simple Majority

Not under Art. 368. Example: Admission of new states (Art. 2), Creation/abolition of State Legislative Councils, Changing names of states, Creating/abolishing UTs.

② Special Majority (Art. 368)

Majority of total membership + 2/3rd of present & voting in each House. Most amendments fall here — Fundamental Rights, Directive Principles, etc.

③ Special Majority + 50% States

Art. 54, 55, 73, 162, Chapter IV of Part V, Chapter V of Part VI, 7th Schedule, Representation of states in Parliament, Art. 368 itself.

⚠️ Basic Structure Doctrine — Kesavananda Bharati (1973)

Supreme Court held that Parliament cannot amend the basic structure of the Constitution. Parliament has constituent power but cannot destroy the identity of the Constitution. Basic features include: Supremacy of Constitution, Democratic republic, Secular character, Separation of powers, Federal character, Judicial review, Rule of law, Fundamental Rights.

Anti-Defection Law — 10th Schedule
📌 52nd Constitutional Amendment, 1985 — 10th Schedule

Added by the 52nd Amendment Act, 1985 under Rajiv Gandhi government. Further amended by 91st Amendment, 2003 (removed provision on merger from 1/3rd to 2/3rd). Administered by the Chairman (RS) / Speaker (LS) — their decision is final (subject to judicial review after Kihoto Hollohan case, 1992).

❌ GROUNDS FOR DISQUALIFICATION
  • Voluntarily gives up membership of political party
  • Votes or abstains from voting contrary to party direction
  • Member of party after election votes against party's whip
  • Independently elected member joins a political party after election
  • Nominated member joins a party after 6 months of taking seat
✅ EXCEPTIONS (NO DISQUALIFICATION)
  • Merger — at least 2/3rd of the legislature party agrees to merge (91st Amendment changed from 1/3rd)
  • Member elected as Speaker or Deputy Speaker may resign from party — no disqualification
  • Member elected as Chairman or Deputy Chairman of RS — same
  • Split was originally an exception (1/3rd members) — removed by 91st Amendment
⚖️ Who Decides? — Kihoto Hollohan Case, 1992
  • Decision taken by Speaker (LS) / Chairman (RS)
  • Earlier: their decision was FINAL (not subject to judicial review)
  • SC in Kihoto Hollohan (1992): Speaker/Chairman's decision IS subject to judicial review by High Courts and Supreme Court
  • But courts can only review after final order — not at preliminary stage
  • SC can review under Art. 32 & 136
📊 Key Numbers & Concepts
  • Merger requires 2/3rd of legislature party members (91st Amendment)
  • Council of Ministers limited to 15% of House strength (91st Amendment)
  • A disqualified member cannot be appointed as minister
  • Whip = Party directive. Defying 3-line whip = disqualification
  • Speaker's decision on Speakership — vacates party membership voluntarily to be neutral
  • Nabam Rebia case (2016) — SC: Speaker facing removal motion cannot hear disqualification petitions
🎯 UPPSC Trick — Remember "2/3 Anti Two-Third"

After 91st Amendment 2003:
Merger exception = 2/3rd of legislature party
Special Majority for Amendment = 2/3rd of present & voting
No-confidence in President = 2/3rd of effective strength of House
CoM cap = 15% of total House strength

🌿 UP Vidhan Parishad (Upper House)
  • UP has Vidhan Parishad — one of only 6 states with upper house
  • 6 States with Vidhan Parishad: UP, Bihar, Maharashtra, Karnataka, Andhra Pradesh, Telangana
  • UP Vidhan Parishad has 100 members
  • Chairman presides — equivalent to Rajya Sabha Chairman
  • 1/6th of seats — Graduate constituencies, Teacher constituencies, etc.
  • Governor nominates 1/6th of members
  • Term: 6 years (1/3rd retire every 2 years)
📋 State Legislative Process vs Parliament
  • State bills follow same 3-Reading procedure
  • No Joint Sitting at state level if deadlock between Vidhan Sabha & Parishad
  • Governor can summon, prorogue, dissolve — equivalent of President's role
  • Governor's assent — can reserve bill for President's consideration (Art. 200)
  • President can withhold assent to state bills — unlike ordinary bills at Centre
  • State cannot legislate on Union List — Centre can on all 3 lists
  • CM is Head of Government, Governor is Constitutional Head

(b) केवल 3
(c) 1 और 2 सही; 3 गलत
(d) सभी सही

(a) यह संविधान के अनुच्छेद 118 में उल्लेखित है
(b) यह न तो संविधान में और न ही संसदीय प्रक्रिया नियमों में उल्लेखित है
(c) यह प्रश्न काल से ठीक पहले होता है
(d) शून्य काल में मतदान किया जा सकता है
✅ सही उत्तर: (b) — Zero Hour एक भारतीय Parliamentary innovation है। यह 1962 में शुरू हुआ। यह न संविधान में है, न Rules of Procedure में। Question Hour के बाद (12 बजे) शुरू होता है। इसमें मतदान नहीं होता।
