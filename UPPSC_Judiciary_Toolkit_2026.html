📜 Important Constitutional Articles — Judiciary
🔑 Supreme Court — Critical Articles (UPPSC MCQ Hotspot)
Art.
32
Right to Constitutional Remedies — "Heart & Soul of Constitution" SC has power to issue writs for enforcement of Fundamental Rights. This right itself is a Fundamental Right. Cannot be suspended except during Emergency (Art. 359). Dr. Ambedkar called it "most important article."
Art.
124
Establishment of Supreme Court SC shall consist of CJI + not more than 33 other Judges. President appoints after consultation with SC judges. Judges retire at 65.
Art.
131
Original Jurisdiction of SC — EXCLUSIVE Disputes between Centre vs State | Between two or more States. NOTE: Not applicable to disputes arising from pre-Constitution treaties.
Art.
132
Appellate Jurisdiction — Constitutional Cases Appeal to SC from HC if HC certifies substantial question of constitutional law.
Art.
136
Special Leave Petition (SLP) — Residuary Appellate Power SC may grant special leave to appeal from ANY judgment/order/decree of ANY court or tribunal in India. DISCRETIONARY. Cannot be filed against Armed Forces Tribunals.
Art.
137
Review of SC Judgments SC can review its own judgments. Review petition to be filed within 30 days. Grounds: Error apparent on face of record; new evidence; any sufficient reason.
Art.
141
Law Declared by SC — Binding on ALL Courts SC decisions binding on ALL courts in India territory. Not binding on SC itself (can depart by larger bench).
Art.
142
SC Power to Do Complete Justice SC may pass such decree/order as necessary for doing complete justice in any cause/matter. THIS IS VERY WIDE — can override statutory laws in some cases. Used in Bhopal Gas case, etc.
Art.
143
Advisory Jurisdiction — President's Reference President may refer questions of law/fact to SC. SC may refuse advisory opinion. Opinion is NOT binding on President. Used: Ram Janmabhoomi, Cauvery Water Dispute etc.
Art.
145
Rules of SC — Procedure SC can make rules for its practice and procedure with President's approval. Minimum 5 judges for substantive questions of constitutional interpretation.
🏛️ High Court — Critical Articles
Art.
214
High Court for every State — Each State shall have a HC. Parliament can establish common HC for two or more states.
Art.
217
HC Judges Appointment — President appoints every HC Judge in consultation with CJI + Governor of State + Chief Justice of HC. Collegium system now applies.
Art.
226
HC Power to Issue Writs — For enforcement of FRs OR any other purpose. Wider than Art. 32. Can issue writs against both state and private persons for FRs. Discretionary remedy.
Art.
227
HC Superintendence over subordinate courts — HC superintends all civil, criminal courts and tribunals throughout the state. Can call records, give directions, frame rules of practice. NOT subject to Art. 136 appeal.
Art.
228
Transfer of Cases to HC — If HC satisfied substantial constitutional question is involved, can withdraw case from subordinate court and decide itself.
⚡ Judicial Review — Key Articles
Art. 13 — Laws inconsistent with FRs are void
Pre-constitutional laws + Post-constitutional laws | Definition of "law" includes ordinances, orders, rules etc. | Basis of Judicial Review in India
Art. 368 — Power to amend Constitution
Parliament's amending power. Checked by Basic Structure Doctrine. Cannot abrogate basic features (Kesavananda Bharati 1973).
Art. 21 — Right to Life (Expanded Scope)
Originally: "No person shall be deprived of life or personal liberty except according to procedure established by law."
After Maneka Gandhi (1978): Includes RIGHT TO LIVE WITH DIGNITY, livelihood, privacy, health, education, environment etc.
Art. 19 — 6 Freedoms
(a) Speech & Expression | (b) Assembly | (c) Association | (d) Movement | (e) Residence | (g) Profession/Trade
Reasonable restrictions: Art. 19(2)-(6)
🔬 Key Doctrines & Concepts
🏛️ Basic Structure Doctrine — Kesavananda Bharati (1973)
Case: Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala | 1973 | 13-Judge Bench (7:6)
Longest bench in SC history | 703 pages judgment | Decided April 24, 1973
🔑 Holding: Parliament can amend ANY provision of Constitution under Art. 368 BUT cannot destroy/abrogate its BASIC STRUCTURE.
Elements of Basic Structure (identified over time):
✓ Supremacy of Constitution
✓ Republican & Democratic form of government
✓ Secular character
✓ Federal character
✓ Separation of Powers
✓ Fundamental Rights
✓ Rule of Law
✓ Judicial Review
✓ Free & Fair elections
✓ Unity & Integrity of nation
✓ Parliamentary system
✓ Independence of Judiciary
✓ Limited power of Parliament to amend
Background — Golak Nath (1967):
SC held Parliament CANNOT amend FR at all. Led to 24th Constitutional Amendment (Art. 368 amended) + 25th Amendment (Art. 31C added) by Indira Gandhi govt.
Why Important for UPPSC:
• Overruled Golak Nath — gave Parliament power to amend FRs
• BUT added "Basic Structure" limit
• Led to Emergency (1975) & 42nd Amendment controversy
• Affirmed in Indira Nehru Gandhi v. Raj Narain (1975) — added "free & fair elections" to basic structure

⚖️ Judicial Review — Concept & Scope
Definition: Power of courts to examine legislative enactments and executive orders to determine if they violate the Constitution. If unconstitutional → VOID.
Basis: Art. 13 (laws inconsistent with FRs void) + Art. 32 + Art. 226 + Art. 246 (Subject matter distribution)
Indian vs USA Review:
India: Limited (scope of Constitution) | USA: Broader (Judge-made law — Marbury v. Madison 1803) | India does NOT have "due process of law" (has "procedure established by law" — Art. 21)
What can be reviewed:
✓ Parliamentary Acts
✓ State Legislature Acts
✓ Constitutional Amendments (if violate basic structure)
✓ Executive actions / Ordinances
✗ Policies of government (unless arbitrary/violate FRs)
Key Case: L. Chandra Kumar (1997)
Judicial review is part of BASIC STRUCTURE of Constitution. Tribunals cannot exclude HC's supervisory jurisdiction under Art. 226/227.
👥 Collegium System — Judicial Appointments
First Judges Case (1981)
S.P. Gupta v. Union of India — Executive primacy in judicial appointments
Second Judges Case (1993)
SC Advocates-on-Record v. Union of India — CJI's recommendation binding; collegium born
Third Judges Case (1998)
Presidential Reference — Collegium = CJI + 4 senior-most SC judges
NJAC Judgment (2015)
99th Amendment + NJAC Act struck down — collegium system reaffirmed
Current Collegium System:
SC Judges: Recommended by CJI + 4 senior-most SC Judges
HC Judges: Recommended by Chief Justice of HC + 2 senior-most HC Judges + consulted by SC Collegium
Transfer of HC Judges: SC Collegium recommendation
NJAC (2015) — Why Struck Down:
National Judicial Appointments Commission included: CJI + 2 senior SC judges + Law Minister + 2 eminent persons. SC held: Independence of Judiciary = Basic Structure; executive in appointments = threat to independence.

🏆 Tier-1: Absolutely Must Know
🔵 Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala
1973 | 13-Judge Bench | Art. 368 | TOP PRIORITY
Issue: Can Parliament amend any part of Constitution including FRs?
Decision: Yes, but Basic Structure cannot be destroyed/abrogated
Significance: Foundation of constitutionalism; limits Parliamentary sovereignty
Legacy: NJAC (2015) struck down using this doctrine
🟠 Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India
1978 | 7-Judge Bench | Art. 21 | TOP PRIORITY
Issue: Passport impounded without hearing — Art. 21 violated?
Decision: "Procedure established by law" must be fair, just & reasonable (not just any procedure)
Significance: Expanded Art. 21; linked Art. 14, 19, 21 together; brought "due process" principle
Overruled: A.K. Gopalan (1950) which read articles in isolation
🟢 Vishaka v. State of Rajasthan
1997 | SC | Sexual Harassment | TOP PRIORITY
Issue: Bhanwari Devi gang rape; no law for workplace sexual harassment
Decision: SC laid down Vishaka Guidelines — employer duty to prevent sexual harassment
Significance: Guidelines had force of law till POSH Act 2013 was enacted
Legacy: Prevention of Sexual Harassment at Workplace Act, 2013
🔴 SR Bommai v. Union of India
1994 | 9-Judge Bench | Art. 356 | TOP PRIORITY
Issue: Validity of President's Rule in various states (Karnataka, MP, HP, Rajasthan)
Decision: President's Rule is SUBJECT to judicial review; floor test must be held; majority test = democracy
Significance: Curbed misuse of Art. 356; Secularism = Basic Structure; states are NOT merely agents of Centre
Legacy: Reduced arbitrary use of Art. 356 from 90+ times to rare occasions
🔵 Indira Sawhney v. Union of India (Mandal Case)
1992 | 9-Judge Bench | OBC Reservation | Art. 16(4)
Issue: 27% OBC reservation validity; creamy layer; 50% ceiling
Decision: 27% OBC reservation valid; 50% ceiling on reservations (except extraordinary circumstances); creamy layer to be excluded; no reservation in promotions
Significance: Established 50% ceiling rule followed till date
2024 Update: Sub-classification within SC/ST now permitted (State of Punjab v. Davinder Singh 2024)

🏛️ Lucknow Bench — Special Feature
Lucknow Bench Established: Under Letters Patent 1948; was seat of Chief Court of Oudh. Area covered: Districts of Avadh/Oudh region including Lucknow, Faizabad (Ayodhya), Barabanki, Sitapur, Hardoi etc.
Status: NOT a separate HC — it is a permanent bench of Allahabad HC. Judgments have equal authority. Cases can be transferred between Prayagraj & Lucknow benches.
Historical Significance: Chief Court of Oudh (1925-1948) was merged into Allahabad HC in 1948. Ram Janmabhoomi (Ayodhya) matters were filed in Lucknow bench — the famous December 6, 1992 + 2010 HC judgment.
Recent SC Intervention on Allahabad HC (2026): SC took suo motu cognizance of Allahabad HC order showing insensitivity in sexual offence case; constituted Expert Committee for judicial sensitivity training.

📢 PIL — Concept, Landmarks & Tribunals
💡 Public Interest Litigation (PIL) — Complete Coverage
What is PIL?
Any member of public (not just aggrieved party) can file petition for public interest. Locus standi is diluted. Even a letter/postcard to SC/HC can be treated as PIL.
Origin: Justice PN Bhagwati & Justice V.R. Krishna Iyer — Early 1980s. Krishna Iyer first used PIL concept. Bhagwati institutionalized it.
Who can file?
Any citizen | NGO | Journalist | Social activist
Even letter to SC (Epistolary jurisdiction)
Where filed?
Supreme Court → Art. 32 | High Court → Art. 226
SC has wide PIL jurisdiction; HC also entertains PILs
Landmark PILs:
  • Hussainara Khatoon (1979) — Undertrial prisoners; speedy trial
  • Bandhua Mukti Morcha (1984) — Bonded labour released
  • Sheela Barse v. Union of India (1986) — Women in police custody
  • MC Mehta series (1987+) — Environment; Ganga pollution; Taj Mahal protection
  • Vishaka (1997) — Sexual harassment guidelines
  • PUCL v. Union of India (2003) — Right to food
  • Vineet Narain (1997) — CBI independence; Hawala case
  • Common Cause v. UOI (2018) — Passive euthanasia
Criticism of PIL:
"PIL Tourism" / "Public Interest Litigation" vs "Private Interest Litigation"
Courts asked to observe judicial restraint | PIL on frivolous matters impose fine
